About

Welcome!

I am a computational materials scientist interested in how atomic-scale mechanisms shape deformation, phase transformations, and environmental degradation in structural alloys.

I completed my Ph.D. in Materials Science & Engineering at the University of Michigan in June 2026, with a joint degree in Scientific Computing. My research integrates theoretical modeling, atomistic simulations, and data-driven methods to understand and design complex alloy systems.

My work focuses on how phase transformations, defects, interfaces, and surface reactions govern mechanical performance and environmental stability. By bridging atomic-scale mechanisms with mesoscale deformation and degradation behavior, I aim to establish physics-based principles and computational tools for advanced alloys with improved strength, ductility, and corrosion resistance.

Research interests

  • Phase transformations in alloys
  • Interface chemistry and surface engineering
  • Defect engineering in structural alloys
  • Advanced computational methods development

Research

Theoretical and computational studies in alloys

Phase transformations and deformation in titanium alloys

I study how phase stability, free-energy landscapes, and local atomic structures control competing deformation modes in metastable beta titanium alloys, including twinning, slip, and transformation-mediated mechanisms.

Defects, interfaces, and corrosion

I investigate how solutes, intermetallic particles, grain boundaries, and oxide surfaces influence cathodic activity, localized corrosion, and environmental stability in alloy systems.

Computational methods for materials design

I develop and use atomistic simulation, free-energy methods, and data-driven modeling to connect mechanisms across scales and support rational alloy design.
